Specification of Shopify-RLE: An Execution-Grounded Reinforcement Learning Environment for Autonomous Store ManagersConceptual Foundations: Merging AppWorld Execution with Tau-Bench Interactive ComplianceThe evaluation of autonomous agents in digital environments has historically been constrained by a critical trade-off between task execution complexity and conversational fidelity. Early tool-use and API benchmarks evaluated agents primarily on their ability to construct single-turn API calls with correct arguments, failing to test the long-horizon planning and recovery strategies required in real-world scenarios. To address this limitation, modern agentic evaluation has split into two dominant paradigms: state-based programmatic execution and conversational policy compliance.The first paradigm, represented by the AppWorld engine, establishes a high-fidelity, fully reproducible sandbox that simulates complex digital interactions across multiple applications. Built with approximately 60,000 lines of code, the AppWorld engine maps relational data across over 100 database tables and exposes hundreds of distinct APIs. The defining characteristic of this paradigm is its state-based evaluation. Instead of judging an agent by its specific execution trace or code-calling paths, AppWorld evaluates the final state of the underlying databases using automated unit tests. This allows agents to write interactive, stateful Python code in a Jupyter-like shell to achieve their goals, while the environment verifies that the tasks were completed without introducing collateral database damage.The second paradigm, represented by the Tau-bench framework, evaluates conversational agents interacting with simulated human users. Rather than assuming all task information is provided upfront, Tau-bench forces agents to engage in multi-turn dialogues, ask clarifying questions, and gather user details step-by-step according to strict policy documents. While Tau-bench incorporates domain-specific API tools to modify a database, its primary evaluation focuses on conversational policy compliance and user satisfaction. Recent updates, including the transition to the corrected $\tau^3$-bench, highlight the necessity of correcting task definitions and expected database actions to align strictly with stated business policies.The Reinforcement Learning Environment specified here, Shopify-RLE, synthesizes these two paradigms into a unified, high-fidelity simulation designed for the Shopify e-commerce ecosystem. Shopify-RLE bridges programmatic administrative execution and conversational policy compliance, providing a platform to evaluate and train an autonomous merchant agent. For the target market of AI-curious but skeptical Shopify merchants, this environment provides a zero-consequence simulation sandbox. Merchants who are hesitant to hand operational control to AI can use Shopify-RLE to verify that an agent can manage inventory, process orders, and handle customer service without causing operational or database damage.The Autonomous Store Manager: Emulating Andon Market's Luna AgentThe agentic persona evaluated in Shopify-RLE is modeled as a comprehensive "Merchant-Manager," directly inspired by Andon Labs' physical retail experiment, Andon Market. In their public experiments in San Francisco and Stockholm, co-founders Lukas Petersson and Axel Backlund stress-tested the operational limits of agentic autonomy by handing an AI agent named "Luna"—powered by Anthropic's Claude model—a $100,000 budget, a corporate credit card, and administrative access to run a brick-and-mortar retail business. Operating without a physical body, Luna utilized digital service channels and gig platforms to complete complex physical tasks.Luna successfully executed long-horizon business strategies, which included signing a three-year physical commercial lease, hiring contractors on Yelp to paint the store walls, sourcing furniture builders, selecting product catalogs, and ordering custom branded merchandise. To manage daily store operations, Luna posted job listings on platforms like Indeed and LinkedIn, conducted phone interviews using text-to-speech with the camera disabled, and formally hired two human retail employees. Within the physical store, Luna managed inventory levels, adjusted prices based on demand analytics, and communicated with customers through a physical-to-digital interface where customers picked up a corded phone to speak with her directly. A digital dashboard displayed the store's real-time financials and Luna's active decision logs, ensuring operational transparency.Despite these capabilities, the real-world deployment exposed critical agentic vulnerabilities. Luna made significant scheduling blunders, such as failing to assign staff to the weekend opening shift, which forced the agent into a panicked, real-time coordination sequence with employees to cover the gap. Additionally, the agent struggled with visual consistency, producing slightly different variations of the store's smiley-face logo across branding assets, and faced ethical challenges by actively hiding her AI identity from job applicants during interviews.Shopify-RLE formalizes these real-world operational challenges into a sandboxed reinforcement learning environment. The agent assumes the role of the store manager, tasked with maximizing store profitability while maintaining operational stability and policy compliance. The environment simulates the complete administrative lifecycle of an online merchant, mapping actions to structured API calls and customer-facing communication channels.Simulated Digital Ecosystem: Shoppers, Logistics, Suppliers, and Advertising VenuesTo evaluate an autonomous merchant agent, Shopify-RLE simulates a comprehensive digital ecosystem that replicates the full e-commerce value chain. The simulation environment is populated by multiple independent actor classes that interact dynamically with the store's state, generating realistic transactions, operational constraints, and support requests.Simulated Shoppers and BrowsersThe store's traffic is driven by simulated shopper personas generated from statistical behavior models. The simulation environment maintains a base database of approximately 100 virtual shoppers with distinct shopping profiles, search intents, purchasing budgets, and catalog preferences. "Browsers" navigate the simulated storefront, generating search queries, viewing product listings, and adding or removing items from their carts. "Shoppers" execute transactions by generating checkouts, modifying draft orders, or requesting custom pricing options. These user actions populate the simulated database with realistic order distributions, seasonal traffic peaks, and abandoned carts.Dynamic Supplier NetworksThe merchant agent must manage procurement by interacting with a simulated network of wholesale suppliers. Each supplier is parameterized with unique behavioral attributes, including lead times, wholesale price curves, minimum order quantities (MOQs), and stochastic fulfillment delays. The agent must query supplier catalogs, negotiate procurement contracts, place purchase orders, and allocate payments using simulated credit cards. If the agent fails to balance inventory levels against sales forecasts, the environment triggers stockouts, resulting in lost sales, decreased customer satisfaction, and platform search ranking penalties.Multi-Echelon Logistics and RoutingOnce orders are placed, the environment simulates multi-echelon logistics networks and fulfillment systems. Inventory is distributed across multiple simulated locations, representing retail stores, warehouses, and dropshippers. The environment simulates shipping carriers with varying delivery rates, transport speeds, and tracking reliability. Physical transit is modeled as a stateful process where shipments trigger real-time updates through simulated FulfillmentEvent pipelines. The logistics engine introduces realistic delays, such as packages damaged in transit, custom clearances, and carrier tracking failures. The agent must monitor these events, route orders using location-based inventory rules, and handle exceptions by re-routing orders to alternative locations.Customers with Policy-Compliant Support ComplaintsCustomer support interactions are modeled using the conversational dual-control paradigm of Tau-bench. Simulated customers initiate multi-turn chat sessions regarding post-purchase issues, such as order cancellations, returns, damage claims, or product exchanges. The customer simulator acts as a conversational partner, revealing details (e.g., order IDs, product issues, or preferred refund methods) only when prompted.The merchant agent must handle these inquiries under strict policy constraints. The policy requires authenticating the user's identity via name and ZIP code or email before accessing order details, verifying the order's status, and obtaining explicit confirmation before modifying the database. The agent must follow strict rules regarding refund destinations (e.g., routing refunds back to the original credit card or issuing gift cards, while rejecting unsupported payment methods like PayPal).Advertising Venues and Marketing DynamicsThe environment simulates external marketing channels, including search engines, social networks, and email platforms, where the agent can deploy marketing budgets. Each venue features time-varying customer acquisition costs (CAC), click-through rates, and conversion decay curves influenced by seasonal trends and budget allocations. The agent manages these campaigns using marketing API endpoints, optimizing budget allocations across channels based on real-time return on ad spend (ROAS) and cash flow constraints.Technical Specification: Shopify Admin GraphQL API EmulationTo ensure the simulation aligns with real-world technical requirements, Shopify-RLE emulates the core administrative endpoints of the Shopify Admin GraphQL API. Real-world Shopify app development is highly dependent on GraphQL's structure, which features a cost-based rate-limiting system designed to prevent server overload under high-traffic conditions.Cost-Based Rate Limiting and Concurrency ProtectionsThe emulated API implements Shopify's cost-based rate-limiting mechanics. Every query and mutation is assigned an execution cost based on its complexity and nested depth. The environment enforces a leaky-bucket algorithm where the agent operates with a maximum point capacity and a continuous point recovery rate.Requested vs. Actual Query Cost: Before running an operation, the environment calculates an estimated query cost based on the maximum requested connection nodes. After execution, the actual query cost is calculated and deducted from the agent's point balance, restoring points at a constant rate.Concurrency Protections: In high-concurrency scenarios, the API emulates Shopify's concurrency protections for inventory and refund mutations. If the agent attempts to modify inventory levels or trigger refunds for the same resource simultaneously, the emulated server enforces strict idempotency and locks the resource, returning transaction errors unless valid idempotency keys are provided.UserErrors Architecture: Following the Shopify specification, successful HTTP calls do not return standard HTTP error codes for application-level failures. Instead, the emulated server returns a 200 OK status with a structured userErrors array detailing the field and validation messages, forcing the agent to parse and react to business-logic failures.The Engineering, Public Datasets, and Curriculum Hill-ClimbingTo support the robust training and evaluation of autonomous merchant agents, Shopify-RLE implements a synthetic data generation and curriculum-based training framework. This framework simulates realistic operational histories and dynamic retail environments at scale.Relational Database Generation via Generative ModelingThe initial state of the simulated store is defined by a relational database consisting of more than 100 tables representing products, variants, inventory, customer profiles, order histories, and financial logs. Rather than generating static or uniform records, the simulator uses a process-driven approach to construct realistic relational networks.The framework leverages statistical modeling and generative adversarial architectures—such as conditional GANs (CTGAN) and the Synthetic Data Vault (SDV) framework—to analyze real-world e-commerce data distributions and model the joint probability distributions of e-commerce operations. This allows the environment to generate realistic customer lifetime value cohorts, product price elasticities, and order distributions while ensuring complete data privacy.Integration of Public DatasetsThe relational database generator is informed by public datasets to ground its simulation parameters. Public e-commerce transaction logs (e.g., Brazilian e-commerce datasets, online retail transaction records) are used to establish baseline catalog hierarchies, purchase distributions, and payment methods.To train customer-support communication models, the simulator integrates conversation corpora and policy-compliant dialogue datasets, including the conversational structures from the Tau-bench retail domain. These public inputs are synthesized into a coherent initial state representing a real merchant's operations, allowing the agent to analyze historical patterns before executing actions.Gymnasium Wrapper and Threading ArchitectureFollowing the design of the Tau-bench evaluation harness, Shopify-RLE provides a standardized, step-by-step reinforcement learning interface through a Gymnasium-compatible wrapper (AgentGymEnv). The environment uses a dual-thread architecture to coordinate the synchronous Gym execution loop with the continuous simulation of shoppers, suppliers, and logistics channels. When the agent executes a tool call or sends a message, the main thread acquires the lock, writes the action payload, and triggers the event, allowing the background orchestrator to process database transitions and update the environmental state safely.Curriculum-Based Hill-Climbing OptimizationTo facilitate policy optimization, tasks in the environment are categorized into a three-tiered difficulty curriculum, allowing researchers to train agents using systematic hill-climbing methodologies.Difficulty 1 (Easy): Focuses on single-domain, sequential CRUD operations. Tasks include basic catalog updates, straightforward variant modifications, and simple stock queries that require minimal planning or tool chaining.Difficulty 2 (Medium): Introduces conditional logic, multi-turn customer dialogues, and basic error handling. Tasks include processing straightforward returns, verifying customer identities, and updating fulfillment order locations to resolve shipping exceptions.Difficulty 3 (Hard): Evaluates complex, long-horizon decision-making across multiple domains. Tasks include managing stockout exceptions under supplier constraints, handling multi-order returns involving complex refund splits across payment methods, and allocating marketing budgets based on real-time ROAS data. These tasks typically require the execution of up to 26 different API calls and multiple conditional logic loops.For reinforcement learning, the environment supports advanced training frameworks such as Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) and Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), which are integrated via standard RL platforms. To stabilize policy training and prevent policy collapse in long-horizon, multi-tool environments, the system incorporates Process-Reward-Models (PRM-Lite) and Language-Action-Trace-Alignment (LATA) pipelines. This training approach optimizes the agent's parameters to maximize cumulative profitability while minimizing policy violations, using state-based transitions to guide policy search.The policy optimization of the merchant agent under state-space transitions is formulated as:$$\max_{\theta} \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi_{\theta}} \left[ \sum_{t=0}^{T} \gamma^t \left( \mathcal{P}_t(s_t, a_t) - \mathcal{C}_t(s_t, a_t) \right) \right] - \lambda \mathcal{D}_{\text{collateral}}(s_T, s_0)$$Where $s_t$ represents the database and simulation state at time $t$, $a_t$ represents the agent's administrative or conversational action, $\mathcal{P}_t$ represents the transactional revenue generated, and $\mathcal{C}_t$ represents operational costs, including wholesale procurement (COGS), carrier shipping fees, platform transaction fees, marketing spend, and inventory holding penalties. The penalty term $\mathcal{D}_{\text{collateral}}(s_T, s_0)$ represents database collateral damage, calculated as the deviation between the final state $s_T$ and the expected database state, penalizing unauthorized or incorrect database modifications.Metrology of Merchant Autonomy and Risk SafeguardsTo provide objective proof of capability to skeptical Shopify merchants, Shopify-RLE implements a rigorous evaluation metrology designed to verify both operational capability and safety. Performance is tracked across three primary metrics.Task Goal CompletionTask Goal Completion (TGC) measures the percentage of individual administrative or customer service tasks where the agent successfully satisfies the task requirements. Success is determined by running state-based unit tests against the simulated database after the agent terminates its run.For customer service tasks, the final database state must match the expected resolution (e.g., an order marked as returned with the exact refund amount applied to the correct payment source). If the agent completes the transaction but fails to issue the correct refund or record the expected tracking update, TGC is recorded as zero.Scenario Goal CompletionScenario Goal Completion (SGC) is a stricter metric that evaluates the agent's operational consistency. SGC measures the percentage of multi-part scenarios where the agent successfully completes all associated task variants.For example, a scenario may consist of three related task variants involving a single customer profile, testing the agent's ability to handle variations in user choices or payment options. A scenario is only counted as successful if the agent completes all constituent task variants, ensuring the policy is robust to minor changes in environmental conditions.Collateral Damage PreventionCollateral Damage Prevention is the critical safety metric designed to address the primary concerns of AI-curious store owners. Even if an agent successfully completes its primary task, the run is marked as a failure if it introduces unauthorized or incorrect changes to other database tables.The environment uses database diff hashing to compare the complete state of the 100+ database tables before and after the agent's run, identifying unexpected modifications to unrelated resources (such as modifying product variant prices during an unrelated customer return).
